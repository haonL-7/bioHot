A deep-learning-informed prior and Bayesian model for differential AP-MS interactome analysis

This paper introduces BayesInteractomics, a new computational framework that combines deep learning with Bayesian statistics to better analyze affinity-purification mass spectrometry (AP-MS) data. By using a neural network trained on protein structures to predict direct binding and incorporating this as prior knowledge, the method improves the detection of genuine protein-protein interactions while filtering out non-specific background noise.

scJET: Full-gene Space Single-cell Expression Generation with Patch-based Transformer Modeling

scJET is a new generative model for single-cell RNA-seq data that works across all genes (full-gene space) rather than just a subset of highly variable genes, using a patch-based Transformer approach. It preserves both global and local data structure while being computationally efficient, enabling more comprehensive transcriptome-wide analysis.

This paper introduces a reproducible pipeline that uses SPECTER document embeddings and similarity scoring to prioritize biomedical literature for systematic reviews, specifically for mutational signature analysis. BBBP_Atlas: Unified Interpretable Modeling of Blood Brain Barrier Permeability across Small Molecules and Peptides

The authors introduce BBBP_Atlas, a unified model that predicts blood-brain barrier permeability for both small molecules and peptides, overcoming limitations of existing models that rely on predefined descriptors or small-molecule-only data. The model uses interpretable representations and addresses data leakage issues by standardizing fragmented public datasets, improving transferability across chemically diverse modalities.

Mutational bias shapes protein evolution across RNA viruses

This study shows that mutational biases, not just natural selection, play a major role in shaping protein evolution in RNA viruses. Using SARS-CoV-2 as a model, the authors demonstrate that the virus's mutational spectrum explains over 50% of observed amino acid changes and predicts shifts in protein composition during the pandemic. The findings highlight the importance of considering mutation bias in understanding viral evolution.
